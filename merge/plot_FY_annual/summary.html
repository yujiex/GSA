Summary of level 1

  1. Count
    1. Total number of buildings in the EUAS data set by category

      2. GSA has 1000 - 1500 buildings in their portfolio with multiple year energy monitoring at some level, including around 700 buildings of category A or I required to meet federal energy mandates (42 U.S.C. 8253 - Energy management requirements)

      According to the EUAS data set, GSA has reduced the total number of buildings in their portfolio by 30% since 2003, and shifted a greater percent to government owned goal buildings (A category) each year.

      • A: government owned buildings subject to the energy reduction goals
      • B: government owned buildings exempt from the goals
      • I: energy intensive buildings (usually, but not always, owned) subject to the goals
      • C: Standard Active Lease
      • D: Exempt Leases
      • E: Reimbursable & Non-Reportable

      This reduction in total building numbers does not corresponds to a reduction of total square footage for the entire GSA portfolio, since on average buildings become larger in size.

      • The total square footage for the whole GSA portfolio increased by 1%.
      • The total square footage increased by 2% for A + C + I category.
      • The total square footage increased by 10% for the A + I category with at least 6 years of Electric EUI >= 12 kBtu/sq.ft and Gas EUI >= 3 kBtu/sq.ft from FY2007 to FY2015
    2. Covered facility by category and type

      3. The majority of the covered facilities are office buildings, followed by courthouses. There are 173 covered facilities within category A and I, and is subject to the energy reduction goal.

    4. Capital vs operational investment building count by year in EUAS data set (All buildings)

      5. To meet federal energy mandates of 30% EUI reductions from 2003 to 2015 for goal buildings (A + I), GSA has invested in energy metering as well as operational, capital and combined operational-capital upgrades to the building stock.

      Almost all covered facilities have some type of investment. There are also 250 to 300 non-covered facilities with investment.

    5. Capital vs operational investment robust vs non-robust energy set

      6. Energy monitoring of GSA facilities has improved over time. However, there remain a significant number of buildings without gas meters, and some with inaccurate electric meters.

    6. Energy investment breakdown

      7. To meet federal energy mandates of 30% EUI reductions from 2003 to 2015, a diversity of operational and capital investments have been made.

  2. Total Energy Trend

    Given the robustness of the energy data sets, and the focus on owned buildings that should meet the federal mandate, the following portfolio conclusions can be drawn

    1. EUI trend of A + I buildings in EUAS data set with at least 6 years of Electric EUI >= 12kBtu/sq.ft and Gas EUI >= 3 kBtu/sq.ft from FY2007 to FY2015 data, only consider Gas + Electric for EUI

      2. While overall square footage of owned GSA facilities (A+I) increased by 10%, total energy use dropped by 16% for a final 2003 to 2015 EUI drop of 24%

    2. EUI trend of A + C + I buildings in EUAS data set using raw Category designation, EUI computed including all energy types

      3. While overall square footage of owned and leased GSA facilities (A+C+I) increased by 2%, total energy use dropped by 21% for a final 2003 to 2015 EUI drop of 23%

  2. Weather Normalized Saving by ECM Actions
    1. Overview

      2. To identify which operational and capital investments made the greatest contribution to energy savings, it is critical to note that buildings with capital investments often were the worst performing in the portfolio leading to the highest absolute savings. Ordered by percent of savings

      • New air handlers (n = 2), controls (n = 1) and HVAC actions (n = 7) resulted in 20-25% electricity savings.
      • GSAlink programs that followed the introduction of advanced metering resulted in just under 20% energy savings across the 2 projects.
      • Combinations of capital and operational programs consistently saved at least 10% of electricity as well as 10% of gas energy use.

    2. Top action groups
      1. Capital

        2. By reviewing individual projects, it is clear that investments in 10 envelope upgrades resulted in energy savings of 8-30% of electric energy demand with two upgrades increasing electric loads. In four projects with robust gas energy records, three of those projects revealed 20-48% reductions in gas energy demand after envelope upgrades! (with one ineffective).
